### C3. Examiner Strategy Synthesis

**What is the examiner doing?**
- **Nephrology:** Heavy emphasis on CLINICAL APPLICATION (50% Level 3-4). The examiner wants students who can recognize AGN/NS from a case description, differentiate them, and manage them step-by-step. They test DEFINITIONS PRECISELY (steroid regimen, hypoglycemia cutoff, HTN percentiles).
- **Endocrinology:** Heavy emphasis on CONCEPTUAL UNDERSTANDING + REPETITION. Rickets asked 5 times in 10 years — the examiner considers this CORE pediatric knowledge. They test neonatal vs childhood feature differentiation (CHT neonatal features, not childhood short stature).
- **Gastroenterology:** Lighter testing (13 questions vs 24 nephrology). Focus on RARE SIGNS (puddle sign), SURGICAL EMERGENCIES (intussusception), and ANATOMY (porto-systemic anastomosis). Less clinical application, more recall + conceptual.

**What student are they filtering out?**
- Students who memorize without understanding MECHANISMS (e.g., why C3 is low in PSGN but normal in IgA)
- Students who confuse AGE GROUPS (neonatal vs childhood features)
- Students who cannot do CLINICAL INTEGRATION (link URI history to AGN)
- Students with imprecise definitions (wrong steroid dose, wrong hypoglycemia cutoff)

**What defines "high-yield" from the examiner's perspective?**
1. **Frequency + Marks:** AGN (6 questions, 42 marks) is the single highest-yield nephrology topic
2. **Clinical relevance to Indian practice:** Rickets (nutritional deficiency common), NS (common pediatric kidney disease), AGN (post-infectious common in tropics)
3. **Differential diagnosis skills:** AGN vs NS, glomerular vs non-glomerular hematuria, steroid classifications
4. **Emergency recognition:** Testicular torsion, intussusception
5. **Public health relevance:** Immunization, ORS, newborn screening (CHT)
