Very High Probability Nephrology Essay / Short Note

A 3-year-old child is brought with puffiness of face and tea-colored urine 10 days after a sore throat. Discuss the diagnosis, investigations, and management. (2+4+4=10)
Definition & Etiology → 2M Clinical Features → 2M Investigations → 2M Management → 2M Complications & Prognosis → 2M

1. Definition & Etiology

Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN) is an immune-mediated inflammatory disease of the glomeruli, most commonly Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN) — the classic "nephritic syndrome" of childhood.

  • Agent: Nephritogenic strains of Group A β-hemolytic Streptococcus (M-types 1, 4, 12, 18, 25, 49, 55, 57, 60)
  • Trigger: Pharyngitis (1–3 weeks prior) or skin infection (impetigo — 3–6 weeks prior)
  • Age: Peak 5–12 years; rare <2 years
  • Pathophysiology: Streptococcal antigens → immune complex deposition (IgG + C3) on GBM → complement activation → neutrophil infiltration → glomerular inflammation → ↓GFR
flowchart TD A["Strep throat or Skin infection"] --> B["Streptococcal antigen release"] B --> C["Immune complex formation (IgG + C3)"] C --> D["Subepithelial deposits (lumpy-bumpy GBM)"] D --> E["Complement activation (C3 consumption)"] E --> F["Glomerular inflammation + neutrophil influx"] F --> G["Decreased GFR = Na+ and H2O retention"] G --> H["Edema + Hypertension + Oliguria"]

2. Clinical Features

Classic Triad (Nephritic Syndrome)

  • Hematuria — smoky/tea-colored (RBC casts pathognomonic)
  • Edema — periorbital, pitting, often sudden
  • Hypertension — due to salt-water retention

Associated Features

  • Oliguria (<300 mL/m²/day)
  • Mild proteinuria (<1 g/day — non-nephrotic)
  • Fever, malaise, anorexia
  • Flank pain (renal capsular stretch)
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy (seizures, headache, visual disturbance)

4. Management

A. General & Supportive

  • Bed rest until hematuria, edema, and hypertension resolve
  • Diet: Salt restriction; fluid restriction to insensible losses + urine output; protein — normal intake
  • Daily monitoring: BP, weight, urine output, fluid balance

B. Treatment of Complications

C. Eradication of Strep

  • Penicillin V 250 mg PO BD/TDS × 10 days (or Benzathine Penicillin G IM single dose)
  • If penicillin allergic: Erythromycin or Azithromycin

5. Complications

  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — reversible; dialysis may be needed
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy — seizures, papilledema, altered sensorium
  • Acute pulmonary edema — due to fluid overload
  • Chronic kidney disease — rare (<1%); associated with crescentic transformation

6. Prognosis

  • Excellent in children; >95% complete recovery
  • Diuresis begins in 5–10 days; hematuria clears in 1–3 weeks
  • Complement normalizes by 6–8 weeks (if not, consider MPGN or lupus)
  • Recurrence is rare (unlike IgA nephropathy)

1. Definition & Classification

Nephrotic Syndrome (NS) is defined by massive proteinuria (>50 mg/kg/day or >40 mg/m²/hr or Urine Protein/Creatinine ratio >2), hypoalbuminemia (<2.5 g/dL), hyperlipidemia, and edema.

Membranous NephropathyRare in childrenSubepithelial depositsSecondary (HBV, SLE, drugs)

Classification by Steroid Response (ISKD)

  • Steroid Sensitive (SSNS): Remission within 4 weeks of prednisolone
  • Steroid Resistant (SRNS): No remission after 4 weeks
  • Steroid Dependent: 2 relapses during steroid taper or within 2 weeks of stopping
  • Frequently Relapsing: ≥2 relapses in 6 months or ≥4 in 12 months

2. Clinical Features

  • Edema — periorbital (worse in morning), dependent (legs by evening), ascites, pleural effusion, scrotal edema
  • Frothy urine — due to massive proteinuria
  • Weight gain — due to fluid retention
  • Anorexia, lethargy, diarrhea — gut edema
  • Hypovolemia signs — cold peripheries, tachycardia, hypotension (despite edema!)
  • Infections — due to loss of IgG and complement factors in urine
  • Thrombosis — loss of antithrombin III; renal vein thrombosis most common

1. Definition

Rickets is a disorder of defective mineralization of the growth plate (physis) in children caused by vitamin D deficiency, calcium deficiency, or phosphate deficiency. Osteomalacia is the adult equivalent affecting trabecular bone.

  • Craniotabes — ping-pong ball sensation of skull (3–6 months)

4. Radiological Findings (X-ray Wrist — AP View)

X-ray of the wrist is the gold standard for diagnosing rickets. Look for changes at the distal radius and ulna metaphysis:

  • Cupping — concavity of metaphysis (saucerization)
  • Fraying — irregular, brush-like appearance of metaphyseal margins
  • Splaying — widening of the growth plate (>2 mm)
  • Reduced bone density — coarsened trabecular pattern
  • Greenstick fractures
Normal Metaphysis
Smooth, sharp, well-corticated margin
Rachitic Metaphysis

B. Monitoring

  • Clinical improvement in 2–4 weeks; radiological healing in 3–6 months
  • Monitor serum Ca, PO4, ALP at 4, 8, 12 weeks
  • 25(OH)D target: >30 ng/mL

C. Surgical

  • Corrective osteotomy for severe deformities (after healing of rickets)

6. Prevention

  • Breastfed infants: 400 IU/day Vit D3 from first few days of life
  • Formula-fed infants: If <1 L/day formula, supplement 400 IU
  • Pregnant & lactating mothers: 600 IU/day
  • Sun exposure: 15–30 min/day (arms & legs)
  • Diet: Fortified milk, eggs, fish, cod liver oil

Describe the clinical features, screening, and management of congenital hypothyroidism. (2+2+1=5)

1. Definition & Etiology

Congenital Hypothyroidism (CH) is thyroid hormone deficiency present at birth. It is the most common preventable cause of intellectual disability. Incidence: 1:2500–1:4000 live births.

TypeCause%
Primary (Thyroid dysgenesis)Agenesis, hypoplasia, ectopic thyroid (lingual/submandibular)80–85%
Primary (Dyshormonogenesis)Defects in thyroid hormone synthesis (TSH receptor, thyroglobulin, peroxidase, pendrin, deiodinase)10–15%
Central (Hypothalamic-Pituitary)Defective TRH/TSH secretion; midline defects (holoprosencephaly, septo-optic dysplasia)1–5%

2. Clinical Features

Neonatal Period (First 2–4 Weeks)

  • Prolonged physiological jaundice (>7 days) — unconjugated
  • Hypotonia, lethargy, poor feeding
  • Constipation
  • Hypothermia (cold, mottled skin)
  • Large anterior + posterior fontanelle
  • Bradycardia
  • Respiratory distress (myxedema of vocal cords)
  • Edema (periorbital, peripheral)

Infantile Period (2–3 Months Onwards)

  • Coarse facies — flat nasal bridge, puffy eyes, macroglossia
  • Umbilical hernia
  • Goiter (in dyshormonogenesis)
  • Developmental delay — poor head control, delayed milestones
  • Hoarse cry
  • Dry skin, sparse hair
  • Short stature
  • Anemia (macrocytic)
Clinical Features — COLD CHILD
C-O-L-D C-H-I-L-D
Constipation  |  Obstructive jaundice (prolonged)  |  Large fontanelle  |  Developmental delay  |  Course facies  |  Hoarse cry  |  Intellectual disability (if untreated)  |  Lethargy  |  Dry skin

3. Newborn Screening

All newborns must be screened at day 3–5 of life (before discharge, optimally 48–72h after birth to avoid physiological TSH surge).

  • Primary screening: TSH (most common) or T4
  • Abnormal: TSH >20–40 mIU/L (varies by lab); T4 <10 μg/dL
  • Confirmatory test: Serum TSH, free T4, total T4
  • If TSH high + T4 low: Primary hypothyroidism
  • If TSH low/normal + T4 low: Central hypothyroidism — do MRI pituitary

4. Management

  • Levothyroxine (L-thyroxine) — 10–15 μg/kg/day PO started immediately after confirmation
  • Start treatment within 2 weeks of life for normal neurodevelopmental outcome
  • Crush tablet, mix with breast milk/formula; do NOT give with soy/iron/calcium (reduces absorption)
  • Monitoring: TSH and free T4 every 1–2 months in first 6 months; then every 3 months until age 3; then every 6–12 months
  • Target: TSH 0.5–2.0 mIU/L; free T4 in upper normal range
  • Prognosis: Excellent if treatment starts within 2 weeks; IQ normal

  • Prognosis: Excellent if early treatment; normal IQ — 0.5M

2. Clinical Features of Testicular Torsion

  • Sudden severe unilateral scrotal pain (often wakes child from sleep)
  • Nausea and vomiting (due to severe pain)
  • High-riding testis (horizontal lie — "bell-clapper deformity")
  • Absent cremasteric reflex (most sensitive sign)
  • Scrotal swelling, erythema, tenderness
  • No relief with elevation (vs epididymitis where elevation helps — Prehn's sign)

3. Investigations

  • Color Doppler USG — test of choice; shows absent or decreased blood flow to testis (sensitivity 90–100%)
  • Radionuclide scan — rarely used now; "cold spot" in torsion
  • Urine analysis — normal (vs pyuria in infection)
  • Do NOT delay surgery for imaging if clinical suspicion is high

A 7-year-old child presents with painless hematuria. Discuss the approach to diagnosis and differential diagnosis. (1.5+1.5=3)

1. Classification of Hematuria

Glomerular Hematuria

  • Color: Cola/tea/smoky (brown)
  • RBCs: Dysmorphic, fragmented (acanthocytes)
  • Casts: RBC casts pathognomonic
  • Proteinuria: Present (often >+++)
  • Clots: Absent
  • Associated: Edema, hypertension, ↓GFR

Non-Glomerular Hematuria

  • Color: Fresh red/pink
  • RBCs: Isomorphic (normal shape)
  • Casts: Absent
  • Proteinuria: Minimal or absent
  • Clots: May be present
  • Associated: Dysuria, flank pain, trauma history

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) is an acute metabolic complication of diabetes mellitus characterized by:

Hyperglycemia
BG greater than 200 mg/dL
Metabolic Acidosis
pH less than 7.30 or HCO3 less than 15 mEq/L
Ketonemia / Ketonuria
Positive serum ketones
  • Pathophysiology: Absolute insulin deficiency leads to increased gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, and decreased glucose uptake. Lipolysis increases, producing free fatty acids that are converted to ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) in the liver, causing metabolic acidosis.
  • Precipitating factors: New-onset T1DM (20-40%), missed insulin doses, infection, trauma, stress
  • Severity: Mild (pH 7.25-7.30), Moderate (pH 7.00-7.24), Severe (pH less than 7.00)

  • 0.9% Normal Saline 10-20 mL/kg over 1-2 hours (bolus only if shock/hypotension)

  • Maximum bolus: 20 mL/kg total (unless in shock)

  • Rate: Replace deficit over 48 hours (slower than adults to prevent cerebral edema)
  • Fluid choice: 0.45-0.9% saline with 5% dextrose once BG falls below 250-300 mg/dL
